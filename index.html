The short version

Everything we do is not that way.

The mobility industry is:

  • Proprietary parts designed to be replaced only through one service chain
  • Prices set by low volume and corporate overhead, not by what things cost to make
  • Devices designed for insurance approval, not for the life you want to live
  • A repair process that takes weeks and requires a phone call to identify a part
  • One predetermined option at one price point — take it or leave it

"Everything we do is not that way."

ParaBoards is a modular mobility system built on standardized, user-serviceable parts available from common vendors. You own it. You can fix it. If ParaBoards LLC closes tomorrow, your rig still works.

The argument

This is a design problem, not a medical one.

The price isn't the problem. The system that produces it is.

Powered mobility devices cost $10,000–$20,000 not because the technology requires it, but because the market is structured around insurance reimbursement, low volume, and corporate overhead. The price is a product of the system, not the components.

Proprietary parts are a choice, not a necessity.

Standardization is proven across dozens of industries. The mobility device industry chose not to use it. When a part fails and the serial number has worn off, users don't have a repair problem — they have an information access problem that was engineered in. ParaBoards uses standardized parts available from common vendors. You can order replacements today.

Electric transport is already affordable for everyone else.

E-bikes start under $1,000 (about $1,000). Electric scooters under $500 (about $500). The technology for affordable outdoor personal transport already exists. The access gap for wheelchair users is a funding decision, not a technical limitation. ParaBoards is designed for the life users want to live, not the one the system decided they get.
